Author’s note —

HIMS is currently sitting at $28.27 with a market cap of $6.5b after rising +10.21% in a single session. Over the last six months, the price has slid due to the regulatory uncertainty surrounding GLP-1's, despite positive financial data (that regulatory risk has since decreased in light of the deal with Novo Nordisk). Revenue has increased 59% YoY and subscriber count has continued to rise. The bear signal through lower price targets from analysts from a financial perspective is due to the slowing-growth of both quarter-on-quarter revenue and subscriber count.

The decelerating growth curve is unlikely to continue, given the acquisition of Eucalyptus, with pro-forma combined revenue being closer to $3.15B compared to management's FY26 guidance of $2.7 - $2.9bn. J.P. Morgans healthcare team, led by Chris Schott, further increased revenue growth sentiment. They highlighted that the GLP-1 cash pay addressable market is over 120M individuals in the US, with only 3M of those currently utilizing compounded or branded GLP-1s. Thus, they are of the belief that HIMS' 2.5m subscriber base has 'ample room for growth.'

Comparing the below valuation techniques point to a low-single digit downside read at the current spot price. The nature of the stock and external factors could have a significant impact of whether these estimates are realized.

HIMS currently has 71m shares sold short, representing roughly 38% of all publicly available shares. At 39% of the float being short, squeeze risk is elevated, and with 2.5 days to cover given the average daily volume, the price could fluctuate on a given catalyst. Also, any synthesis based on the P/E multiples is impacted through the rate of intangibles meaning that both earnings and invested capital are understated, weakening the signal of the multiple. External factors such as the resolution of the FDA peptide meeting in July could see new revenue streams open up for HIMS that have yet to be priced into potential revenue growth. Analysis from a qualitative perspective provides greater insight into how the price could react moving forward.

All three techniques converge on the same conclusion: HIMS is priced for execution. The DCF base case gives downside of 5.7%; the peer-multiples ratio analysis confirms the premium can only be justified by sustained 20%+ growth and successful margin ramp to 18%; and the sell-side is already there - 14 analyst mean target of $26.04 sits 8% below spot, meaning the market is currently pricing HIMS BARELY above where the consensus has it. The DCF carries the most weight here because (i) loss-making peers like TDOC, LFMD and AMWL make multiples-based comps unstable, and (ii) the sensitivity grid makes the value-driver tradeoffs explicit: at WACC <= 9% AND terminal g >= 3%, spot is justified; everywhere else it isn't. The Eucalyptus integration (closing 2H 2026) is the swing variable not captured in the explicit-period growth path - if it compounds at announced triple-digit rates, the FY27-28 growth assumptions are too low and the DCF understates intrinsic value. art-7 art-10

Growth drivers

Weight-loss / GLP-1 subscriber economics

Personalized GLP-1 plans drove 20.9 lbs (10.3% of body weight) average loss within 6 months and 75% adherence at 6 months, supporting the personalization moat HIMS uses to defend ARPU against branded competition art-5. The launch and subsequent regulatory reset of compounded semaglutide is the single largest swing factor in the trailing comps; the Q1 2026 pivot to branded GLP-1s is its next chapter art-9 art-10.

Subscriber growth + ARPU expansion

Subscribers grew from 2.439M (Q2 2025) to 2.584M (Q1 2026), but the engagement story inflected: monthly online revenue per average subscriber expanded through 2025 (+19% YoY in Q3) before turning negative in Q1 2026 (-6% YoY to $80) as the branded-GLP-1 mix and compounded wind-down reset unit economics art-3 art-10.

Gross-margin trajectory and mix

Gross margin moved 76% (Q2 2025) -> 74% (Q3) -> 72% (Q4) -> 65% (Q1 2026), the step-down in the final quarter reflecting the branded-GLP-1 cost mix and restructuring tied to the US weight-loss strategic shift, on top of the early dilution from international acquisitions (Zava, Livewell) art-2 art-10.

International expansion as the next growth leg

Management embedded at least $200M of international revenue contribution in the FY2026 outlook before any Eucalyptus contribution, signaling that the Zava (Europe), Livewell, and pending Eucalyptus rollups are now load-bearing for the growth algo as US compounded-GLP-1 normalizes art-4 art-7.

Operating leverage and cash generation

FY2025 net income of $128M and Adjusted EBITDA of $318M (14% margin) on $2.348B revenue confirmed HIMS had crossed into structural profitability — but Q1 2026 printed a $92.1M GAAP net loss as restructuring and the branded pivot compressed Adjusted EBITDA margin to 7%, a near-term reset management frames as the cost of repositioning art-4 art-10.

FDA de-risking + the peptide personalization optionality

In February 2026, HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy signaled that the FDA will move to reclassify 14 peptides from Category 2 to Category 1 — clearing the legal path for them to be compounded and distributed through HIMS's telehealth platform. Moving substances into Category 1 lets 503B facilities compound them without needing a shortage trigger to legalize the practice. Back in 2025, HIMS bought a California peptide manufacturing facility, which at the time was framed as a GLP-1 supply hedge. With this latest development, they are clearly positioning for a peptide personalization line spanning metabolic optimization, cognitive performance and recovery science. TAM expansion could be a key driver in H2 2026.
