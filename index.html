Author’s note —

HIMS is currently sitting at $28.27 with a market cap of $6.5b after rising +10.21% in a single session. Over the last six months, the price has slid due to the regulatory uncertainty surrounding GLP-1's, despite positive financial data. Revenue has increased 59% YoY and subscriber count has continued to rise. The bear signal through lower price targets from analysts from a financial perspective is due to the slowing-growth of both quarter-on-quarter revenue and subscriber count.

The decelerating growth curve is unlikely to continue, given the acquisition of Eucalyptus, with pro-forma combined revenue being closer to $3.15B compared to management's FY26 guidance of $2.7 - $2.9bn. J.P. Morgans healthcare team, led by Chris Schott, further increased revenue growth sentiment. They highlighted that the GLP-1 cash pay addressable market is over 120M individuals in the US, with only 3M of those currently utilizing compounded or branded GLP-1s. Thus, they are of the belief that HIMS' 2.5m subscriber base has 'ample room for growth.'

Comparing the below valuation techniques point to a low-single digit downside read at the current spot price. The nature of the stock and external factors could have a significant impact of whether these estimates are realized.

HIMS currently has 71m shares sold short, representing roughly 38% of all publicly available shares. At 38% of the float being short, squeeze risk is elevated, and with 2.5 days to cover given the average daily volume, the price is highly volatile on given significant catalysts. Also, any synthesis based on the P/E multiples is impacted through the rate of intangibles meaning that both earnings and invested capital are understated, weakening the signal of the multiple. External factors such as the resolution of the FDA peptide meeting in July could see new revenue streams open up for HIMS that have yet to be priced into potential revenue growth. Analysis from a qualitative perspective provides greater insight into how the price could react moving forward.

Growth drivers

Weight-loss / GLP-1 subscriber economics

Personalized GLP-1 plans drove 20.9 lbs (10.3% of body weight) average loss within 6 months and 75% adherence at 6 months, supporting the personalization moat HIMS uses to defend ARPU against branded competition art-5. The launch and subsequent regulatory reset of compounded semaglutide is the single largest swing factor in 2025 quarterly comps art-9.

Subscriber growth + ARPU expansion

Subscribers grew from 2.4M (Q1) to 2.511M (Q4), a 13% YoY exit rate, while monthly online revenue per average subscriber expanded materially (+53% YoY in Q1, +19% YoY in Q3) — meaning revenue growth is being driven more by deepening engagement than raw subscriber adds art-1 art-3.

Gross-margin trajectory and mix

Gross margin moved 73% (Q1) -> 76% (Q2) -> 74% (Q3) -> 72% (Q4); FY2025 gross margin was 74%, down from FY2024 levels, reflecting the cost mix of compounded GLP-1 fulfillment and the early dilution from international acquisitions (Zava, Livewell) art-2 art-4.

International expansion as the next growth leg

Management embedded at least $200M of international revenue contribution in the FY2026 outlook before any Eucalyptus contribution, signaling that the Zava (Europe), Livewell, and pending Eucalyptus rollups are now load-bearing for the growth algo as US compounded-GLP-1 normalizes art-4 art-7.

Operating leverage and cash generation

FY2025 net income of $128M and Adjusted EBITDA of $318M (14% margin) on $2.348B revenue confirms HIMS has crossed into structural profitability — material because the Eucalyptus deal's cash-at-close is being funded internally rather than via dilution art-4.

FDA de-risking + the peptide personalization optionality

In February 2026, HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy signaled that the FDA will move to reclassify 14 peptides from Category 2 to Category 1 — clearing the legal path for them to be compounded and distributed through HIMS's telehealth platform. Moving substances into Category 1 lets 503B facilities compound them without needing a shortage trigger to legalize the practice. Back in 2025, HIMS bought a California peptide manufacturing facility, which at the time was framed as a GLP-1 supply hedge. With this latest development, they are clearly positioning for a peptide personalization line spanning metabolic optimization, cognitive performance and recovery science. TAM expansion could be a key driver in H2 2026.

04 · BULLISH CATALYSTS

Catalysts strengthening the bull case

Two recent developments that have moved decisively in HIMS' favour: the FDA's resolved stance on compounded Category 1 peptides, and the Hims × Novo Nordisk partnership-then-settlement that converted a courtroom rival into a sourcing channel.

FDA Category 1 peptides

Intro

The FDA's compounded-peptide regime tightened sharply through 2024-2025 — 19 peptides moved to Category 2 in late 2023, semaglutide came off the shortage list on Feb 21 2025 and tirzepatide on Dec 19 2024, ending the legal basis for mass 503A compounding by Apr 22 / Feb 18 2025 respectively art-1 art-3 art-4. HHS Secretary RFK Jr.'s February 2026 reversal — restoring 14 peptides to Category 1 ahead of a July 2026 PCAC review — partially re-opens the personalisation lane that drove HIMS' weight-loss segment to a $725M-plus 2025 run rate, though branded GLP-1s remain explicitly outside that relief art-5 art-6 art-7. Net read: regulatory tail risk has narrowed but not retired — the directional fork hinges on the July PCAC vote and any FDA action on the 503B bulks list excluding semaglutide/tirzepatide/liraglutide art-8.

Timeline

FDA places 19 peptides on Category 2 'do not compound' list

FDA's late-2023 reclassification swept popular peptides (BPC-157, CJC-1295, ipamorelin, others) into Category 2, blocking 503A pharmacies from compounding them under the FD&C Act art-5.

FDA refers five peptides to PCAC

CJC-1295, ipamorelin acetate, thymosin alpha-1, AOD-9604 and selank acetate were formally referred to the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee for review on the 503A bulks list art-5.

FDA declares tirzepatide shortage resolved

FDA issued a Declaratory Order resolving the tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) shortage; 503A enforcement discretion ended Feb 18 2025 and 503B on Mar 19 2025 art-3.

FDA declares semaglutide shortage resolved

FDA's Declaratory Order ended the legal basis for mass-compounding semaglutide; 503A pharmacies had until Apr 22 2025 and 503B facilities until May 22 2025 to wind down art-1 art-2.

Outsourcing Facilities Association sues FDA

OFA filed in N.D. Texas challenging FDA's semaglutide shortage resolution as 'arbitrary and capricious'; court denied preliminary injunction on Apr 24 2025, upholding FDA's determination art-2.

HHS Secretary RFK Jr. moves to restore 14 peptides to Category 1

RFK Jr. publicly announced the reversal of roughly 14 of the 19 Category 2 peptides, restoring legal compounding pending PCAC review art-5 art-6.

FDA schedules July 2026 PCAC peptide review

FDA noticed PCAC meetings July 23-24 2026 covering BPC-157, KPV, TB-500, MOTS-C (Day 1) and Emideltide/DSIP, Semax, Epitalon (Day 2) for 503A bulks-list inclusion art-5.

FDA proposes excluding GLP-1s from 503B Bulks List

FDA published a proposal to permanently exclude semaglutide, tirzepatide and liraglutide from the 503B Bulks List — closing the 503B compounding lane even if shortages recur art-8.

Commentary

AUTHOR’S TAKEAWAY

The FDA peptide regime has branched into two distinct files for HIMS: 1) GLP-1 compounding is structurally closed — the Apr 30 2026 503B Bulks List proposal would lock that door even on a future shortage, removing the single most consequential pathway for compounded semaglutide revenue art-8; 2) the broader peptide universe (BPC-157, ipamorelin, thymosin alpha-1) is paradoxically opening up after RFK Jr.'s February 2026 meeting, giving HIMS a credible path to a peptide personalisation product line outside the GLP-1 products art-5. The investable read on today's +10% move is the market pricing a higher probability of favourable July PCAC outcomes. Favourable outcomes will lead to TAM expansion for HIMS, providing it with a bullish catalyst.

Bull case

If July 2026 PCAC votes restore even a subset of the 14 reclassified peptides to the 503A bulks list, HIMS gains a regulatorily-clean adjacent personalisation category that can absorb part of the lost compounded-GLP-1 revenue art-5.

Bear case

FDA's Apr 30 2026 proposal to exclude semaglutide/tirzepatide/liraglutide from the 503B Bulks List signals durable hostility to GLP-1 compounding even on shortage events, capping any reversion in the highest-margin weight-loss line art-8.

Hims × Novo Nordisk

Intro

The Hims-Novo relationship has cycled through three distinct phases inside 13 months: an Apr 29 2025 NovoCare Pharmacy collaboration to sell branded Wegovy at a $599/mo bundled price (HIMS +20% on the print), a Jun 23 2025 unilateral termination by Novo over alleged 'illegal mass compounding and deceptive marketing' (HIMS -34%, ~$5B market cap evaporated), and a Mar 9 2026 settlement that re-opens the channel with FDA-approved Ozempic and Wegovy (injection + oral) and dismisses Novo's patent suit art-1 art-2 art-3 art-7. The settlement is asymmetric: HIMS gives up compounded-GLP-1 advertising and Novo retains the right to refile, but HIMS preserves a clinically-justified compounding carve-out — making Wegovy access a genuine offset to the 503A wind-down rather than a replacement for it art-7 art-8. Today's +10.2% move likely reflects building conviction that the multi-source GLP-1 strategy (Novo branded + Lilly Zepbound/Mounjaro added Apr 2025 + limited compounding) is durable through Q2 2026 art-7 art-9.

AUTHOR'S TAKEAWAY

Across the four calls, management's framing has recalibrated on the themes most exposed to regulatory and margin risk.
